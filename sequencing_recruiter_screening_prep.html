"When I joined Snagajob, all the marketing was essentially employer-facing. Job seekers were getting a handful of transactional messages — application confirmations, that kind of thing — but there was no lifecycle strategy, no segmentation, no onboarding sequence.

I started with personas. The prior mental model was basically 'they all need a job,' which is true but not useful. I did research to understand how workers actually thought about their job searches — their confidence levels, their preferences, their relationship with the platform. What came out of that was a much clearer picture of different intent profiles, and it immediately changed how I thought about messaging.

From there I mapped what behaviors we wanted to encourage and what onsite signals predicted readiness for them. One thing I identified early was that most users signed up to apply for a single specific job — not to establish an ongoing relationship with a platform. That insight shaped the entire onboarding sequence. The job wasn't just to confirm their account — it was to actively make the case for why coming back had value: building out their profile, using search, setting up alerts. You had to articulate a reason to return before they'd decided there wasn't one.

Right out of the gate, the series was generating about $6.40 in revenue per 1,000 emails delivered — compared to $0.41 per 1,000 for the existing non-lifecycle job merchandising email program. So even the early baseline was about 15x more efficient. And it didn't end with launch — I kept testing and optimizing, so the ROI actually increased by 2.5x over the next nine months.

What I'm most proud of isn't the numbers, it's the foundation. The personas became core to how the broader team prioritized campaigns. The segmentation logic underpinned the predictive campaign I built later that drove 70% more return visits than average. Starting from real behavioral insight rather than demographic assumptions paid off across everything that came after it."
"The principle I start from is that messaging needs to lead with the benefit in a way that makes the user recognize themselves in it. When that lands, they lean in — that's when you have permission to go deeper into the detail. If you lead with the mechanism or the technical explanation, you've lost most people before you get to the part they actually care about.

A good example of this is a program I rebuilt at Conduent. Every year, a chunk of new HSA account holders failed a regulatory identity verification step — which meant their accounts couldn't be funded until they resolved it. The existing outreach was dry, legalistic, and generic. It explained the regulation, listed what was required, and that was it. Response rates were around 5%.

When I revamped the program, the content transformation was the whole thing. Instead of leading with the compliance requirement, I led with the personal consequence — and I made it specific to each person's situation. If your employer was trying to put money into your account, the message was: your employer is trying to give you money and can't. If you were self-funding, it was: you're losing the tax benefit you signed up for. The required action was also personalized to the actual failure type — most people just needed to confirm a physical address, which is a thirty-second fix, but the old messaging didn't distinguish that from cases that required ID documents. The specificity made it feel solvable rather than bureaucratic.

Activation rate went from 5% to 27%.

The underlying principle is the same whether you're writing about identity verification requirements or genomic health reports: people engage when the message is about their situation, not about the system. The science or the regulation is the proof — it's never the lede."
"I've worked with Segment, though not in a full CDP deployment — at TurboTenant we used it to sync between our product and Klaviyo. When I led the migration from Klaviyo to Customer.io, we moved away from Segment entirely and built a direct Postgres sync instead. I was the business process owner on that: I drove decisions about what data came across, which attributes we used for identity, sync frequency, and how we revised the sync logic as accuracy issues surfaced.

Identity resolution was one of the real challenges. In Klaviyo with Segment, we could define contact identity on the intersection of two attributes — a stable customer ID and an email address. Customer.io required a single identity attribute. A product architecture decision outside of marketing meant we ended up using email as the unique identifier, which caused sync problems whenever users changed email addresses or transferred account access. That's a direct lesson in why identity resolution is a foundational decision — the downstream effects show up everywhere in your audience logic.

On the behavioral side, I've done a lot of what Segment's computed traits do, just inside Customer.io. I worked with engineering on event taxonomy — defining events like 'fire when a user adds their third property,' designing the payload that comes with it, and using those events to trigger campaigns or build audience segments. I built fairly sophisticated segment logic in Customer.io: rules like 'this event fired twice in the last three days,' combined with attribute conditions, to target users at specific lifecycle moments. That's the same underlying work as computed traits and audience building in a CDP — the tool boundary is just different.

Segment as a full CDP — centralized identity resolution, audience syncing across destinations — is territory I'd be ramping into. But I already understand the data problems it's solving because I've dealt with those problems directly. I'd expect a fast ramp on the tooling."

The higher-leverage tests in my experience aren't usually about copy. They're structural: is this message reaching the right segment? Is it arriving at the right lifecycle moment? Is the behavioral trigger actually predicting what I think it's predicting? That said, copy does matter, especially at the headline level. The best copy decisions come from understanding what users actually care about and how they think about the product — which is really just another form of the same hypothesis-driven work. When I test copy, I'm usually testing a positioning assumption, not just a word choice."

"Each channel has a different set of tradeoffs, and I think about them along a few dimensions: persistence, real estate, reach, interruptiveness, and user tolerance for promotional content.

Email is my anchor channel for most lifecycle work. It has the highest reach, it's the least interruptive, and messages are persistent — a user can come back to an onboarding email three days later when they're actually ready to act on it. The tradeoff is inbox competition and the risk of accumulating annoyance if you overmessage.

In-product messaging is powerful when your targeting is precise, because you can reach someone exactly when the content is contextually relevant. But it's highly interruptive, usually not persistent — if the user dismisses it, it's gone — and it's a bad channel for reaching people who aren't already in the product frequently. I think of it as ideal for contextual tips, not for driving people back to the product.

SMS has real estate constraints and users have high expectations that messages are genuinely value-added. Low tolerance for anything that feels promotional without a clear reason. I think it works best when timeliness is the actual value — when the user needs to know something now. At TurboTenant, our first SMS campaign came from that exact insight. We were notifying landlords of maintenance issues via email, but a tenant reporting a water leak on Friday afternoon could mean a missed email, a weekend plumber, and compounding damage. I launched SMS for maintenance notifications — email still went out 15 minutes later as a backup — and A/B tested against email-only. The test group responded about 10% more often overall, and 25% more often on weekends. That's the kind of use case where SMS earns its place.

Push is the channel I'm most cautious about. Zero persistence, so I always pair it with a backup in another channel. Reach is hit-or-miss since many users never grant permission — but the ones who do are initially very receptive, which makes it easy to overuse and erode trust quickly. Deep linking also usually requires engineering resources to do properly. A lot of companies get push wrong by treating it like a broadcast channel. I think of it as a high-trust channel that has to be earned and carefully maintained.

The through-line across all of it is: the channel choice should follow the nature of the message, not the other way around. What does this message need to do? How urgent is it? Does the user need to be able to return to it? How tolerant is this segment of interruption? The answers usually point pretty clearly to the right channel — or combination."
